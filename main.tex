\begin{abstract}
Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X) communications increasingly rely on Multi-access Edge Computing (MEC) to meet stringent low-latency requirements. However, the complexity of Abstract Syntax Notation One (ASN.1) parsers, specifically those utilizing Octet Encoding Rules (OER), introduces a critical vulnerability to algorithmic complexity attacks (CWE-674). Maliciously crafted messages with deep structural nesting can exhaust parser resources, leading to Head-of-Line (HoL) blocking and severe Quality of Service (QoS) degradation for legitimate traffic. This paper proposes a Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker for QoS Preservation, utilizing an Adaptive Finite State Machine (FSM) and an $\mathcal{O}(N)$ structural frequency anomaly detector. By shifting the defense to a lightweight sampling mechanism, our approach achieves low inspection overhead during nominal operations while providing millisecond-level mitigation under attack. Our harness-based evaluation demonstrates that the proposed hybrid defense reduces P99 tail latency from 0.2040 ms to 0.0379 ms, ensuring system stability even under high-intensity structural attacks.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The rapid evolution of Cooperative Intelligent Transport Systems (C-ITS) has established Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X) communication as a cornerstone for future autonomous and semi-autonomous driving. Safety-critical applications demand ultra-low-latency message exchange, typically requiring end-to-end delays below 10 ms. While Multi-access Edge Computing (MEC) architectures are proposed to offload complex computational tasks, the presentation-layer processing remains a potential bottleneck and security vector.

V2X messages, including Cooperative Awareness Messages (CAM) and Decentralized Environmental Notification Messages (DENM), are defined using Abstract Syntax Notation One (ASN.1) and often encoded with Octet Encoding Rules (OER) for high processing efficiency. However, the support for recursive structures in OER introduces significant security challenges. Specifically, ASN.1 parsers are susceptible to algorithmic complexity attacks (CWE-674). By crafting OER-encoded payloads with deeply nested sequences or choice-based recursive structures, an adversary can trigger infinite recursion or exponential object tree expansion. In an emulated edge-processing workload, such "structural bombs" can exhaust CPU resources, leading to Head-of-Line (HoL) blocking. This results in severe latency amplification for legitimate safety messages queued behind a malicious packet undergoing expensive parsing, potentially compromising the safety of the vehicular network.

Conventional security mechanisms often introduce a deterministic "inspection tax" that persists even during nominal operations. For processing high-volume V2X traffic, this continuous overhead can degrade system jitter and inflate P99 tail latency, which is critical for real-time safety requirements. There is a fundamental conflict between the requirement for exhaustive security verification and the need for low-overhead packet forwarding.

To address this challenge, we propose a \textit{Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker for QoS Preservation}. Unlike traditional static filters, our approach utilizes an Adaptive Finite State Machine (FSM) coupled with an $\mathcal{O}(N)$ structural frequency anomaly detector. By leveraging the structural repetition discrepancy between legitimate OER-encoded certificate payloads and the repetitive structural padding required for recursion-based attacks, the system dynamically modulates its inspection intensity. During peace time, the circuit breaker minimizes overhead through probabilistic sampling, transitioning to full-scale inspection only when a structural anomaly is detected.

\section{Background and Threat Model}

\subsection{Background: V2X, ASN.1, and OER}

V2X communication systems rely on a strictly defined protocol stack where the Facilities layer handles the generation and parsing of standardized messages such as CAM and DENM payloads. These messages are defined using ASN.1, a flexible interface description language that supports complex data types and recursive structures. To achieve the high processing performance required for vehicular environments, these messages are frequently encoded using OER.

In future MEC-enabled architectures, edge servers will be responsible for the presentation-layer parsing of ASN.1 payloads. OER recursive structures are common in V2X certificates and security headers (e.g., IEEE 1609.2). For example, a certificate may contain a sequence of attributes that allow for deeply nested object trees. Under CWE-674 attacks, an adversary exploits these recursive definitions to trigger parser-level complexity amplification. A small malicious payload can force the parser to perform thousands of recursive operations, consuming disproportionate CPU cycles.

In emulated edge-processing pipelines, this resource exhaustion manifests as HoL blocking. Legitimate, time-sensitive safety messages queued behind a malicious OER payload suffer from severe latency jitter, even if the MEC node is not volumentrically saturated.

\subsection{Threat Model}

The primary objective of this research is to mitigate parser-layer resource exhaustion attacks in OER-encoded ASN.1 streams.

\subsubsection{Scope of Attack}
The threat model focuses on ASN.1 structural complexity attacks (e.g., CWE-674). The adversary is assumed to be a compromised or malicious OBU or a roadside entity capable of injecting syntactically valid but structurally malicious OER-encoded messages into the network. These messages exploit recursive structures to force the parser into deep recursion or exhaustive memory allocation. 

The attacker's goal is not necessarily to crash the emulated edge node but to introduce sufficient latency jitter to disrupt safety-critical V2X applications. By carefully crafting the nesting depth, the attacker can maximize the "first-miss" impact where the malicious payload is partially processed before being rejected or causing a timeout.

\subsubsection{Attacker Capabilities and Constraints}
1) \textit{Structural Manipulation:} The attacker can manipulate the OER tree depth to exceed typical operational limits.
2) \textit{Structural Pattern Constraint:} Constructing deeply nested OER structures within maximum transmission unit (MTU) limits often necessitates repetitive byte patterns (structural padding) to satisfy length determinants and choice indices. 
3) \textit{Network Layer:} The attack is carried out at the application/presentation layer but manifests as a resource exhaustion denial-of-service (DoS) during the parsing phase. 

\subsubsection{Out-of-Scope Boundaries}
To maintain a focused research scope, the following attack vectors are explicitly considered out-of-scope:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Volumetric DDoS:} We do not address general L3/L4 flooding attacks (e.g., UDP floods) aimed at saturating network bandwidth.
    \item \textbf{Cryptographic Compromise:} We assume the underlying cryptographic primitives (e.g., ECDSA) and PKI structures are secure; the attack targets the parser before or during certificate verification.
    \item \textbf{Replay and Sybil Attacks:} While critical in V2X, these are handled by the security envelope and MIS (Misbehavior Detection) layers, not the parser-layer circuit breaker.
    \item \textbf{PKI/CA Compromise:} Attacks targeting the root of trust or certificate issuance process are beyond the scope of presentation-layer defense.
\end{itemize}

\section{Attack Characterization}

Algorithmic complexity attacks at the presentation layer exploit the deterministic expansion of encoded bit-streams into in-memory object trees. In the context of OER, recursive definitions—such as a \texttt{SEQUENCE OF} containing further nested choice fields—compel the parser to perform repeated memory allocations and pointer dereferences.

An OER "recursion bomb" is constructed by deliberately maximizing this nesting depth. However, because V2X networks enforce strict maximum transmission unit (MTU) limits, an adversary cannot simply append infinite data. Instead, achieving deep recursion within a constrained byte footprint necessitates the repetitive use of structural markers, such as length determinants and specific choice indices. This structural repetition forms the basis of the amplification factor: a payload of merely a few hundred bytes can force the parser to execute thousands of CPU cycles, creating a severe latency bottleneck.

A representative structural repetition pattern observed in our emulated workloads involves long sequences of identical structural octets (e.g., \texttt{0x02} for specific choice markers), which contrast with the high-variation bit-streams of legitimate signed certificates. By identifying the repetitive structural padding inherent to these attacks, our repetition-based detector circumvents the need for complex semantic analysis.

\section{Proposed Mechanism}

To balance the requirement for extreme security against CWE-674 structural attacks with the stringent low-latency constraints of V2X communications, we propose a two-tiered defense architecture: the \textit{Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker}. The system consists of a lightweight $\mathcal{O}(N)$ structural anomaly detector and an Adaptive FSM that dynamically modulates the inspection intensity. This design ensures that the system remains resilient to resource exhaustion without imposing a continuous "inspection tax" on legitimate, time-sensitive traffic.

\subsection{System Overview}
The proposed mechanism is designed to reside at the network edge, specifically within the presentation-layer ingress of an emulated MEC node. The core philosophy is to decouple the expensive ASN.1 parsing operation from the initial packet ingestion. By performing a rapid, structural pattern-aware scan before the payload reaches the recursive parser, the system can identify and discard "structural bombs" with minimal computational overhead. The Adaptive FSM acts as the supervisory logic, utilizing threat intelligence from the anomaly detector to switch between low-overhead sampling and high-assurance strict inspection.

\subsection{O(N) Structural Frequency Anomaly Detector}

Legitimate V2X messages exhibit significant bit-stream variation across their bit-streams. Statistically, long sequences of identical bytes are highly improbable in these OER payloads. Conversely, structural attacks in OER-encoded ASN.1 rely on repetitive structural padding (e.g., repeating choice indices or length determinants) to construct deeply nested object trees within MTU limits. 

Algorithm \ref{alg:filter} defines the proposed structural anomaly detector. Unlike signature-based systems, this detector monitors the frequency of consecutive identical bytes, providing a generalized defense against mutated recursion payloads.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{O(N) Structural Frequency Anomaly Detector}
\label{alg:filter}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $Payload$: Raw byte array of the incoming packet
\Require $T_{thresh}$: Threshold for consecutive identical bytes (e.g., 10)
\Ensure \textbf{True} if anomalous, \textbf{False} otherwise
\If{$Payload$ is empty}
    \State \Return \textbf{False}
\EndIf
\State $Count \gets 1$
\State $CurrentByte \gets Payload[0]$
\For{$i = 1$ \textbf{to} $length(Payload) - 1$}
    \If{$Payload[i] == CurrentByte$}
        \State $Count \gets Count + 1$
        \If{$Count > T_{thresh}$}
            \State \Return \textbf{True} \Comment{Structural anomaly detected}
        \EndIf
    \Else
        \State $CurrentByte \gets Payload[i]$
        \State $Count \gets 1$
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \Return \textbf{False}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Computational Complexity}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Time Complexity $\mathcal{O}(N)$:} The algorithm performs a single pass over the payload. For typical V2X messages ($N \approx 500$ bytes), execution time is sub-microsecond.
    \item \textbf{Space Complexity $\mathcal{O}(1)$:} The detection is performed in-place with constant memory overhead, avoiding the allocation-related jitter common in DPI.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Adaptive Circuit Breaker FSM}

Continuous inspection of all packets introduces a persistent processing delay, which we define as the "inspection tax." To minimize this tax during nominal operations, we implement an Adaptive Circuit Breaker FSM (Fig. \ref{fig:fsm_diagram}).

\subsubsection{State Transitions and Dynamics}
The FSM (Algorithm \ref{alg:fsm}) maintains system throughput during \textit{Peace Time} by sampling only a fraction ($R_{sample}$) of traffic. The primary operational modes are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{PEACE\_TIME:} The system operates in a low-overhead monitoring state. This probabilistic sampling ensures that the system preserves its high-throughput processing capability, effectively eliminating the background inspection tax during normal conditions.
    \item \textbf{UNDER\_ATTACK:} Upon detection of a structural anomaly by the $\mathcal{O}(N)$ detector, the "circuit breaker" triggers. The FSM transitions to this high-assurance state, enforcing 100\% strict inspection for all packets and initiating a cooldown timer ($C_{max}$). 
\end{itemize}

The transition back to \textit{PEACE\_TIME} occurs when the cooldown period expires without further incident. Any subsequent anomaly detection resets the timer, ensuring persistent protection during sustained attacks.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Adaptive Circuit Breaker FSM}
\label{alg:fsm}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $Packet$: Incoming packet
\Require $R_{sample}$: Sampling rate during peace time (e.g., 5\%)
\Require $C_{max}$: Cooldown threshold (e.g., 10000 packets)
\State \textbf{Global State:} $State \in \{PEACE, ATTACK\}$
\State \textbf{Global Variables:} $Cooldown \gets 0$, $TotalPackets \gets 0$

\State $TotalPackets \gets TotalPackets + 1$
\State $DropPacket \gets$ \textbf{False}

\If{$State == PEACE$}
    \IF{$TotalPackets \pmod{(100 / R_{sample})} == 0$}
        \State $DropPacket \gets$ \textsc{AnomalyDetector}($Packet$)
        \IF{$DropPacket ==$ \textbf{True}}
            \State $State \gets ATTACK$
            \State $Cooldown \gets C_{max}$
        \ENDIF
    \ENDIF
\ElseIf{$State == ATTACK$}
    \State $DropPacket \gets$ \textsc{AnomalyDetector}($Packet$)
    \If{$DropPacket ==$ \textbf{True}}
        \State $Cooldown \gets C_{max}$
    \Else
        \State $Cooldown \gets Cooldown - 1$
        \IF{$Cooldown \le 0$}
            \State $State \gets PEACE$
        \ENDIF
    \ENDIF
\EndIf
\State \Return $DropPacket$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{FSM Complexity and Inspection Tax Minimization}
The FSM introduces negligible overhead, requiring only a modulo operation ($\mathcal{O}(1)$) and a conditional branch per packet during peace time. The overall system time complexity dynamically scales between $\mathcal{O}(1)$ (for unsampled packets) and $\mathcal{O}(N)$ (for inspected packets). By using an FSM with probabilistic sampling, we amortize the inspection cost, minimizing the background resource footprint and ensuring the stability of safety-critical V2X services.

\subsection{First-Miss Penalty and Hybrid Defense}

A critical finding in our system jitter analysis is the "First-Miss Penalty." This phenomenon occurs when a structural attack packet is injected during the \textit{PEACE\_TIME} sampling window but is not selected for inspection (95\% probability). This packet reaches the parser, triggering a high-latency event before the FSM can transition to the \textit{UNDER\_ATTACK} state. In the \textit{Unpatched + Filter} configuration, this resulted in a Max latency spike of 3.37 ms (Table \ref{tab:master}).

The \textit{Patched + Filter (Hybrid)} configuration (Mode 2) represents the most resilient architecture. By combining the edge-layer circuit breaker with the application-layer patch, we achieve the lowest overall Mean latency (0.0160 ms). The circuit breaker handles over 90\% of the adversarial load with low overhead, while the official patch serves as a fallback to mitigate the impact of any packets that bypass the probabilistic sampling window. This defense-in-depth approach ensures that the emulated workload remains stable even under stateful adversarial traffic designed to probe sampling gaps.

\section{Discussion}

The experimental results validate the efficacy of the proposed Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker in maintaining deterministic QoS under structural complexity attacks. However, a robust systems-security evaluation requires a nuanced discussion of the mechanism's boundaries, inherent trade-offs, and practical deployment considerations within future MEC environments.

\subsection{System Jitter and Inspection Tax}
Traditional defenses require line-rate DPI, which imposes a constant "inspection tax" on every packet, increasing jitter for safety-critical V2X services. By using an FSM with probabilistic sampling, we amortize the inspection cost. During peace time, the effective complexity is reduced from $\mathcal{O}(N)$ to $\mathcal{O}(R_{sample} \times N)$, which is approximately $\mathcal{O}(0.05N)$, minimizing the background resource footprint. This low-overhead filtering approach is critical for high-throughput edge nodes where compute resources are heavily contested.

\subsection{Tail Latency Control}
In V2X, the P99 tail latency is often more critical than the mean. Structural attacks specifically target the "tail," causing extreme outliers that can disrupt cooperative awareness. Our results show that the circuit breaker mechanism effectively clamps the tail latency, preventing HoL blocking from cascading through the parser processing pipeline. By discarding OER structural bombs before recursive expansion, the system ensures that legitimate time-sensitive packets are processed deterministically.

\subsection{False Positives and Generalizability Limitations}
The $\mathcal{O}(N)$ anomaly detector relies on the statistical improbability of long sequences of identical bytes in high-variation payloads like signed OER certificates. While highly effective for ASN.1 OER, this heuristic may exhibit false positives if applied to protocols that natively utilize extensive structural padding or zero-byte initialization. The repetition threshold ($T_{thresh}$) must be carefully tuned to the empirical distribution of the target protocol. Furthermore, this mechanism is strictly a parser-layer defense; it does not provide universal protection against semantic logic flaws, cryptographic vulnerabilities, or broad volumetric DDoS attacks.

\subsection{The First-Miss Penalty and Hybrid Defense Strategy}
A fundamental limitation of any probabilistic sampling defense is the "first-miss penalty." As observed in the \textit{Unpatched + Filter} scenario, stateful adversarial traffic can exploit the sampling gap during \textit{PEACE\_TIME}, resulting in a high-latency event before the FSM transitions to \textit{UNDER\_ATTACK}. To address this, we strongly advocate for a hybrid defense strategy. Deploying the edge-layer circuit breaker to filter the vast majority of the adversarial load, while maintaining application-layer static recursion limits as a fallback, provides optimal parser pipeline resilience. This ensures that the rare packet bypassing the sample window is caught by the deterministic parser constraint, effectively suppressing the maximum latency spike.

\subsection{Stateful Adversarial Behavior and Deployment Feasibility}
Advanced adversaries might employ "low-frequency" or stateful attack patterns designed to trigger the circuit breaker just frequently enough to maintain the \textit{UNDER\_ATTACK} state, thereby forcing the node to incur the $\mathcal{O}(N)$ inspection tax continuously. However, as demonstrated in our complexity analysis, the $\mathcal{O}(N)$ operation is computationally inexpensive and non-allocating. Therefore, even under a sustained stateful attack, the system experiences only a minor baseline shift rather than severe HoL blocking. The practical feasibility of deploying this mechanism in MEC-enabled V2X ingress pipelines is high, given its minimal memory footprint and compatibility with existing protocol stacks.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}
Internal validity may be impacted by the choice of the 5\% sampling rate and the 10,000-packet cooldown period, which were empirically determined for the evaluated OER workloads. External validity is bounded by the use of the Vanetza protocol stack and the specific certificate structures defined in IEEE 1609.2. While the repetition-based detection logic is generalized for OER recursive patterns, its performance on non-OER encoding rules remains a subject for future verification.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

While the proposed Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker effectively mitigates structural complexity attacks, several limitations present avenues for future research.

First, the $\mathcal{O}(N)$ anomaly detector relies on a statically defined repetition threshold ($T_{thresh}$). While effective for OER, which typically exhibits high bit-stream variation in legitimate cryptographic payloads, applying this heuristic to other protocols or encoding rules may require dynamic threshold tuning to prevent false positives. Future work will investigate adaptive thresholding mechanisms based on real-time traffic analysis.

Second, while the current implementation operates efficiently within the software ingress pipeline, the initial $\mathcal{O}(N)$ scan could be further optimized. Migrating the structural pattern detection to hardware-accelerated environments, such as FPGA smartNICs or eBPF/XDP frameworks, could provide true line-rate filtering before the packets reach the user-space processing queue.

Finally, sophisticated adversaries may attempt to bypass the defense using "low-frequency" stateful attacks designed to stay below the probabilistic sampling rate. Investigating advanced state-tracking algorithms that correlate structural anomalies across extended temporal windows remains a critical area for ongoing security engineering.

\section{Conclusion}

The integration of MEC into V2X networks introduces critical vulnerabilities at the presentation layer, where algorithmic complexity attacks can exhaust parser resources and induce severe Head-of-Line (HoL) blocking. This paper presented a Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker designed to protect V2X infrastructure from ASN.1 OER structural complexity attacks. By combining an $\mathcal{O}(N)$ repetition-based anomaly detector with an Adaptive Finite State Machine, we demonstrated a defense mechanism that preserves QoS while maintaining low inspection overhead during nominal operations.

Our harness-based evaluation confirmed that the proposed Hybrid Defense strategy reduces P99 tail latency from 0.2040 ms to 0.0379 ms under high-intensity structural attacks, effectively mitigating the "First-Miss Penalty" associated with probabilistic sampling. This systems-security approach provides a practical, low-overhead framework for securing high-throughput V2X processing pipelines against parser-layer resource exhaustion.
