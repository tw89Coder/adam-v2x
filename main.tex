\begin{abstract}
Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X) communications increasingly rely on Multi-access Edge Computing (MEC) to meet stringent low-latency requirements. However, the complexity of Abstract Syntax Notation One (ASN.1) parsers, specifically those utilizing Octet Encoding Rules (OER), introduces a critical vulnerability to algorithmic complexity attacks (CWE-674). Maliciously crafted messages with deep structural nesting can exhaust parser resources, leading to Head-of-Line (HoL) blocking and severe Quality of Service (QoS) degradation for legitimate traffic. This paper proposes a Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker for QoS Preservation, utilizing a Probabilistic Risk Budget Finite State Machine (PRB-FSM) and an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ bounded-cost Streaming Structural Risk Filter (SSRF). By shifting the defense to a lightweight sampling mechanism, our approach achieves low inspection overhead during nominal operations while providing millisecond-level mitigation under attack. Our harness-based evaluation demonstrates that the proposed hybrid defense reduces P99 tail latency from 0.1793 ms to 0.0320 ms, transforming recursive parser exhaustion from an unbounded computational
amplification problem into a bounded-cost inspection problem suitable for
MEC-constrained V2X systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The rapid evolution of Cooperative Intelligent Transport Systems (C-ITS) has established Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X) communication as a cornerstone for future autonomous and semi-autonomous driving. Safety-critical applications demand ultra-low-latency message exchange, typically requiring end-to-end delays below 10 ms. While Multi-access Edge Computing (MEC) architectures are proposed to offload complex computational tasks, the presentation-layer processing remains a potential bottleneck and security vector.

V2X messages, including Cooperative Awareness Messages (CAM) and Decentralized Environmental Notification Messages (DENM), are defined using Abstract Syntax Notation One (ASN.1) and often encoded with Octet Encoding Rules (OER) for high processing efficiency. However, the support for recursive structures in OER introduces significant security challenges. Specifically, ASN.1 parsers are susceptible to algorithmic complexity attacks (CWE-674). By crafting OER-encoded payloads with deeply nested sequences or choice-based recursive structures, an adversary can trigger infinite recursion or exponential object tree expansion. In an emulated edge-processing workload, such "structural bombs" can exhaust CPU resources, leading to Head-of-Line (HoL) blocking. This results in severe latency amplification for legitimate safety messages queued behind a malicious packet undergoing expensive parsing, potentially compromising the safety of the vehicular network.

Conventional security mechanisms often introduce a deterministic "deterministic inspection overhead" that persists even during nominal operations. For processing high-volume V2X traffic, this continuous overhead can degrade system jitter and inflate P99 tail latency, which is critical for real-time safety requirements. There is a fundamental conflict between the requirement for exhaustive security verification and the need for low-overhead packet forwarding.

To address this challenge, we propose a \textit{Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker for QoS Preservation}. Unlike traditional static filters, our approach utilizes a Probabilistic Risk Budget FSM (PRB-FSM) coupled with an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ Streaming Structural Risk Filter (SSRF). By shifting the security objective from perfect packet classification to \textbf{bounded computational exposure}, we effectively contain adversarial workloads before they penetrate the resource-intensive parsing stage. The system dynamically modulates its inspection intensity, ensuring that the MEC node maintains deterministic QoS even when subjected to targeted structural complexity amplification.

\section{Background and Threat Model}

\subsection{Background: V2X, ASN.1, and OER}

V2X communication systems rely on a strictly defined protocol stack where the Facilities layer handles the generation and parsing of standardized messages such as CAM and DENM payloads. These messages are defined using ASN.1, a flexible interface description language that supports complex data types and recursive structures. To achieve the high processing performance required for vehicular environments, these messages are frequently encoded using OER.

In future MEC-enabled architectures, edge servers will be responsible for the presentation-layer parsing of ASN.1 payloads. OER recursive structures are common in V2X certificates and security headers (e.g., IEEE 1609.2). For example, a certificate may contain a sequence of attributes that allow for deeply nested object trees. Under CWE-674 attacks, an adversary exploits these recursive definitions to trigger parser-level complexity amplification. A small malicious payload can force the parser to perform thousands of recursive operations, consuming disproportionate CPU cycles.

In emulated edge-processing pipelines, this resource exhaustion manifests as HoL blocking. Legitimate, time-sensitive safety messages queued behind a malicious OER payload suffer from severe latency jitter, even if the MEC node is not volumentrically saturated.

\subsection{Threat Model}

\subsubsection{Adversary Capabilities}
We assume a computationally bounded adversary $\mathcal{A}$ capable of generating syntactically valid but semantically malicious ASN.1 OER payloads. The adversary can:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Construct recursively nested ASN.1 structures to induce worst-case parser complexity.
    \item Generate byte-level patterns designed to evade naive heuristic detectors (e.g., alternating-byte or low-entropy sequences).
    \item Transmit messages within protocol-compliant V2X channels without being blocked by IEEE 1609.2 cryptographic mechanisms.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Adversary Objective}
The adversary aims to maximize edge-node computational latency $L$ by inducing excessive parsing overhead, specifically targeting:
$$L = L_{queue} + C_{parse} + C_{inspection}$$
where $C_{parse}$ dominates under recursive payload structures.

\subsubsection{System Assumptions and Security Goal}
We assume that cryptographic verification is satisfied prior to parsing, and the MEC node operates under a strict latency budget $L \le 10$ ms. The objective of the proposed system is to ensure that worst-case inspection overhead is mathematically bounded:
$$\forall P_i \in \mathcal{P}, \quad C_{inspection}(P_i) \le \mathcal{O}(1)$$
and to prevent adversarial escalation of parser workload prior to ASN.1 decoding.

\subsubsection{Scope of Attack}
The threat model focuses on ASN.1 structural complexity attacks (e.g., CWE-674). The adversary is assumed to be a compromised or malicious OBU or a roadside entity capable of injecting syntactically valid but structurally malicious OER-encoded messages into the network. These messages exploit recursive structures to force the parser into deep recursion or exhaustive memory allocation. 

The attacker's goal is not necessarily to crash the emulated edge node but to introduce sufficient latency jitter to disrupt safety-critical V2X applications. By carefully crafting the nesting depth, the attacker can maximize the "first-miss" impact where the malicious payload is partially processed before being rejected or causing a timeout.

\subsubsection{Attacker Capabilities and Constraints}
1) \textit{Structural Manipulation:} The attacker can manipulate the OER tree depth to exceed typical operational limits.
2) \textit{Structural Pattern Constraint:} Constructing deeply nested OER structures within maximum transmission unit (MTU) limits often necessitates repetitive byte patterns (structural padding) to satisfy length determinants and choice indices. 
3) \textit{Network Layer:} The attack is carried out at the application/presentation layer but manifests as a resource exhaustion denial-of-service (DoS) during the parsing phase. 

\subsubsection{Out-of-Scope Boundaries}
To maintain a focused research scope, the following attack vectors are explicitly considered out-of-scope:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Volumetric DDoS:} We do not address general L3/L4 flooding attacks (e.g., UDP floods) aimed at saturating network bandwidth.
    \item \textbf{Cryptographic Compromise:} We assume the underlying cryptographic primitives (e.g., ECDSA) and PKI structures are secure; the attack targets the parser before or during certificate verification.
    \item \textbf{Replay and Sybil Attacks:} While critical in V2X, these are handled by the security envelope and MIS (Misbehavior Detection) layers, not the parser-layer circuit breaker.
    \item \textbf{PKI/CA Compromise:} Attacks targeting the root of trust or certificate issuance process are beyond the scope of presentation-layer defense.
\end{itemize}


\section{Related Work}

\subsection{V2X Security and Misbehavior Detection}
Security in Vehicular Ad-Hoc Networks (VANETs) relies heavily on public key infrastructure and certificate management to guarantee message authenticity, integrity, and non-repudiation. The IEEE 1609.2 standard establishes the foundational security envelope for V2X communications, utilizing elliptic curve cryptography (ECC) to facilitate robust digital signatures \cite{Farooq2019}. Beyond these foundational cryptographic primitives, Misbehavior Detection Systems (MDS) are extensively deployed to identify semantic anomalies, such as impossible kinematic jumps or position ghosting \cite{vanderHeijden2019}, often relying on local plausibility mechanisms \cite{MBD2019}. Recent comprehensive surveys highlight a strong shift toward machine-learning-based MDS for next-generation autonomous vehicles \cite{Shah2026}. However, existing MDS architectures predominantly operate at the application layer, inherently assuming that the underlying payload has been successfully and safely parsed. Our research addresses a critical vulnerability in the presentation layer that necessarily precedes semantic analysis, securing the parsing pipeline before application-level MDS logic is invoked.

\subsection{Algorithmic Complexity Attacks in Protocol Parsers}
The security of protocol parsers is a long-standing concern in telecommunications, particularly regarding algorithmic complexity attacks that exploit worst-case execution paths to trigger resource exhaustion \cite{Wustholz2017}. In the context of tree-structured data formats, these vulnerabilities frequently manifest as spatial algorithm complexity issues, where deeply nested recursive structures exhaust available memory and computational cycles \cite{Wang2024}. The complexity of parsing modern 3GPP and L2/L3 protocol structures has necessitated automated structural analysis tools \cite{ASN2025}. Recent vulnerability research has increasingly focused on fuzzing ASN.1-based telecommunication protocols, demonstrating that mutating structural boundaries before full compilation is highly effective at uncovering deep logic flaws \cite{ASN_Fuzz2024}. Unlike volumetric DoS attacks that saturate network bandwidth, parser-layer vulnerabilities require minimal traffic to inflict severe computational degradation. By explicitly targeting the repetition-based nature of these structural padding patterns, we position our work as a presentation-layer pre-parsing defense against recursive ASN.1 OER exploitation.

\subsection{MEC QoS Constraints and Edge Security}
The integration of Multi-access Edge Computing (MEC) into V2X architectures introduces stringent operational constraints. Offloading computational tasks to MEC nodes significantly minimizes latency in three-tier V2X architectures \cite{Mach2017, MEC_V2X_2022}, but introduces extreme sensitivity to processing delays \cite{MEC_V2X_2025}. Traditional deep packet inspection (DPI) for complex presentation-layer formats introduces continuous processing delays, inflating baseline jitter and compromising the Quality of Service (QoS) required by vehicular applications. To address these overheads, modern edge security paradigms increasingly leverage programmable in-kernel packet processing mechanisms—such as the extended Berkeley Packet Filter (eBPF) and eXpress Data Path (XDP)—which enable high-speed filtering prior to user-space network stack traversal \cite{Okafor2024, SmartNIC_2019}. Recent advancements have successfully offloaded even complex intrusion detection autoencoders directly into eBPF traces to preserve edge QoS \cite{eBPF_IDS_2026}. Our proposed architecture aligns with these high-performance constraints by implementing a lightweight, probabilistic inspection model that dynamically regulates security overhead, ensuring deterministic QoS under adversarial edge environments.

\section{Attack Characterization}

Algorithmic complexity attacks at the presentation layer exploit the deterministic expansion of encoded bit-streams into in-memory object trees. In the context of OER, recursive definitions—such as a \texttt{SEQUENCE OF} containing further nested choice fields—compel the parser to perform repeated memory allocations and pointer dereferences.

An OER "recursion bomb" is constructed by deliberately maximizing this nesting depth. However, because V2X networks enforce strict maximum transmission unit (MTU) limits, an adversary cannot simply append infinite data. Instead, achieving deep recursion within a constrained byte footprint necessitates the repetitive use of structural markers, such as length determinants and specific choice indices. This structural repetition forms the basis of the amplification factor: a payload of merely a few hundred bytes can force the parser to execute thousands of CPU cycles, creating a severe latency bottleneck.

A representative structural repetition pattern observed in our emulated workloads involves long sequences of identical structural octets (e.g., \texttt{0x02} for specific choice markers), which contrast with the high-variation bit-streams of legitimate signed certificates. By identifying the repetitive structural padding inherent to these attacks, the SSRF-based detector circumvents the need for complex semantic analysis.

\section{Bounded Risk-Aware Pre-Parsing Defense (BRPPD)}

To balance extreme security against CWE-674 structural attacks with the stringent low-latency constraints of V2X communications, we propose a bounded-cost pre-parsing framework. The system guarantees computationally deterministic inspection prior to ASN.1 decoding.

\subsection{Bounded Streaming Structural Risk Filter (B-SSRF)}
Incoming packets are processed using a constant-time streaming sketch that estimates structural instability by calculating the localized second frequency moment ($F_2$) of byte frequencies within a sliding window. This metric, denoted as $SQ$, serves as a collision concentration statistic:
$$SQ = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} c_j^2$$
where $c_j$ represents the frequency of byte value $j$ in the window. For legitimate high-entropy V2X payloads, the distribution of $c_j$ is dispersed, resulting in a low $F_2$ value. Conversely, OER recursion bombs rely on concentrated structural markers (e.g., choice indices and length determinants) to induce complexity within MTU constraints, significantly inflating the $F_2$ moment. This behavior is closely related to the second frequency moment ($F_2$)
commonly used in streaming algorithms to estimate collision concentration
within bounded memory\cite{Alon1996, Krishnamurthy2003}. Recursive ASN.1 payloads exhibit highly concentrated
local symbol distributions due to repeated structural expansion markers,
causing the localized $F_2$ statistic to increase significantly compared
to legitimate CAM/DENM traffic with naturally dispersed byte distributions.

Algorithm \ref{alg:bssrf} defines this bounded filter. Importantly, the correctness and bounding guarantees of the framework rely strictly on the mathematical properties of the $F_2$ estimator, completely avoiding brittle, protocol-specific byte offset signatures. By evaluating localized structural variance rather than static patterns, the detector maintains robust generalizability against evasive MTU-compliant recursion bombs.

\begin{algorithm}[!ht]
\caption{Bounded Streaming Structural Risk Filter (B-SSRF)}
\label{alg:bssrf}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Payload $P$, window size $w$, scanning limit $L_{max}$, threshold $\theta$
\State Initialize frequency sketch $H[256] \gets 0$
\State Initialize sliding window $W$
\State $SQ \gets 0$ \Comment{Localized $F_2$ moment}
\State $n_{scan} \gets \min(|P|, L_{max})$

\For{$i = 0$ to $n_{scan} - 1$}
    \State $b \gets P[i]$
    \If{$|W| = w$}
        \State $b_{old} \gets W.\text{pop\_front}()$
        \State $SQ \gets SQ - (2H[b_{old}] - 1)$
        \State $H[b_{old}] \gets H[b_{old}] - 1$
    \EndIf
    \State $W.\text{push\_back}(b)$
    \State $H[b] \gets H[b] + 1$
    \State $SQ \gets SQ + (2H[b] - 1)$ \Comment{Amortized $F_2$ update}

    \If{$|W| = w$ and $SQ > \theta$}
        \State \Return \textbf{True} \Comment{Structural anomaly detected}
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \Return \textbf{False}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

This stage ensures $\mathcal{O}(w)$ total processing time per packet, where $w$ is the constant window size (e.g., 64 bytes). By limiting the scan to $L_{max}$ bytes, the inspection overhead is strictly $\mathcal{O}(1)$ relative to the total packet length $N$, preventing adversaries from inflating inspection time with large payloads.

\subsection{Probabilistic Risk Budget FSM (PRB-FSM)}

To prevent resource exhaustion without inducing severe baseline jitter, our PRB-FSM dynamically adapts the inspection rate based on current risk budgets, drawing inspiration from adaptive sampling in DPI monitoring \cite{Sosnowski2021} and the software circuit breaker pattern \cite{Nygard2007}.

The system state is governed by a continuous risk budget $B_t \in [0, 100]$, which regulates the probabilistic sampling rate. Traditional discrete Finite State Machines (FSMs) are brittle under stateful adversarial probing. Instead, we define the budget update as:
$$B_{t+1} = \min(B_{max}, \max(0, B_t - \mathcal{P}_t + \gamma_{streak}))$$
where $\mathcal{P}_t$ is the penalty incurred by anomalous structural risk, and $\gamma_{streak}$ represents an adaptive recovery rate. To handle high-intensity burst attacks, the system employs a streak-accelerated recovery mechanism: after a threshold of consecutive clean packets, the recovery rate $\gamma$ is increased (e.g., $6\times$ acceleration) to rapidly restore QoS once the threat dissipates.

The budget thresholds $\{\tau_1, \tau_2, \tau_3\}$ map the continuous budget to discrete operational states ($S_0 \dots S_3$) with escalating sampling rates (10\%, 50\%, 100\%). This mapping provides an auditable and certifiable safety model for V2X systems, ensuring that nominal operations incur minimal overhead ($\sim$10\% inspection) while providing rapid, risk-aware escalation to full deterministic inspection under adversarial pressure.

\subsection{Bounded Inspection Constraint}
By coupling the B-SSRF with the risk budget, we ensure that the inspection cost $C_{inspection}$ for any given packet $P_i$ is bounded:
$$C_{inspection}(P_i) = \begin{cases} \mathcal{O}(1) & R_i \le \theta \\ \mathcal{O}(k) & R_i > \theta, \; k \ll N \end{cases}$$
Thus, the worst-case computational exposure is strictly bounded to $C_{max} = \mathcal{O}(k)$, ensuring $\mathbb{E}[L] \le 10$ ms.


\section{Experimental Setup}

To evaluate the effectiveness and performance overhead of the proposed Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker, we developed a controlled harness-based evaluation framework built around the Vanetza-based test harness, emulating a high-throughput presentation-layer workload. The evaluation methodology focuses on measuring parsing latency, jitter, and system resilience under varying attack intensities.

\subsection{Hardware and Software Environment}
The experimental testbed relies on a dedicated server equipped with a 12th Gen Intel Core i7-12700H processor (20 threads) and 16 GB of RAM, running Ubuntu 22.04 LTS (Kernel 6.6.87 WSL2) to emulate the presentation-layer processing of an edge node.

The software stack is built upon Vanetza, an open-source implementation of the ETSI C-ITS protocol suite % TODO: Add citation for Vanetza. 
Vanetza handles the processing of incoming CAM and DENM payloads, utilizing the underlying \texttt{asn1c} compiler for ASN.1 OER decoding. We developed a custom testing framework, \texttt{qos-harness}, integrated directly into the Vanetza ingress pipeline to inject traffic and precisely measure presentation-layer latency.

\subsection{Traffic Generation and Attack Modalities}
We evaluated the system using a mix of legitimate V2X traffic and malicious CWE-674 recursion payloads. 
Baseline legitimate V2X traffic, comprising standard CAM and DENM payloads, was sourced directly from the reference packet corpus provided by the Vanetza fuzzing toolset to ensure protocol compliance.
To emulate a compromised OBU injecting structural attacks, we generated malicious OER payloads designed to exploit deep nesting vulnerabilities within the IEEE 1609.2 certificate structures. The attack traffic was interleaved with legitimate traffic at varying injection rates (1\%, 5\%, and 10\%) to simulate different threat intensities.

To comprehensively assess the defense mechanisms, we defined three primary operational configurations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mode 0 (Unpatched Native):} The baseline Vanetza parser operating without any recursion-depth limits or edge-layer pre-filtering. This represents the maximally vulnerable state.
    \item \textbf{Mode 1 (Official Patch):} The parser updated with application-layer security patches (e.g., static recursion depth limits in \texttt{asn1c}), but lacking the proposed edge-layer circuit breaker.
    \item \textbf{Mode 2 (Hybrid Defense):} The proposed defense-in-depth architecture, combining the Mode 1 application-layer patch with our $\mathcal{O}(N)$ Adaptive Circuit Breaker FSM at the network edge.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics and Methodology}
The primary objective of the emulated edge node is to maintain deterministic processing times for safety-critical messages. Therefore, our evaluation focuses on micro-benchmarking the parser's execution time per packet.

We recorded the processing latency for millions of injected packets. To analyze the systemic impact of structural attacks, we extracted the following metrics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mean and Median Latency:} To assess the baseline "deterministic inspection overhead" and nominal processing overhead.
    \item \textbf{P99 Tail Latency:} To quantify the worst-case processing delays experienced by the 99th and 99.9th percentile of packets, which is critical for evaluating HoL blocking severity.
    \item \textbf{Max Latency:} To measure the absolute peak delay, particularly relevant for observing the "first-miss" impact before the FSM transitions to the \textit{UNDER\_ATTACK} state.
\end{itemize}

Jitter analysis was conducted by observing the variance in the P99 tail latency distribution. By clamping the P99 metrics, we can quantitatively demonstrate the circuit breaker's ability to preserve QoS and prevent severe jitter in the emulated processing pipeline.

\subsection{Parameter Calibration and Sensitivity Analysis}

The operational efficacy of the PRB-FSM is governed by the risk budget thresholds $\{\tau_1, \tau_2, \tau_3\}$ and the corresponding sampling rates. These parameters were calibrated empirically based on the latency-security tradeoff observed in the test harness. 

A critical consideration in probabilistic security is the handling of sparse, low-intensity threats (e.g., a 1\% adversarial injection rate). Under such conditions, an overly aggressive budget recovery mechanism risks premature "sleep" cycles, creating temporal windows where "first-miss" penalties accumulate. To counter this, we calibrated the clean streak threshold to 1,000 packets and the nominal state ($S_0$) sampling rate to 10\%. 

This hyperparameter tuning ensures that the FSM demands a statistically significant period of absolute network stability before lowering its defensive posture. Consequently, the system maintains elevated risk states during intermittent attack gaps, effectively clamping the False Negative Rate (FNR) to $<0.01\%$ even under the sparsest attack distributions, all without relying on rigid, hardcoded signatures.

\subsection{Evaluation Rigor and MEC Suitability}

To ensure statistical rigor, latency measurements were aggregated across 30 independent execution runs, effectively marginalizing system noise and OS scheduler interrupts. The reported 95\% confidence intervals confirm the stability of the P99 tail latency across millions of processed packets. The MEC suitability of the proposed mechanism is demonstrated by its minimal memory footprint (a 256-entry frequency sketch) and its ability to maintain millisecond-level stability even under a 10\% adversarial load ratio. By clamping the tail distribution, the PRB-FSM ensures that safety-critical V2X applications receive deterministic QoS, satisfying the ultra-low latency requirements of 5G-enabled vehicular networks.

\subsection{First-Miss Penalty and Hybrid Defense}

A critical finding in our system jitter analysis is the "First-Miss Penalty." This phenomenon occurs when a structural attack packet is injected during the probabilistic sampling window but is not selected for inspection. This packet reaches the parser, triggering a high-latency event before the PRB-FSM can deplete its budget and transition to a higher risk state. In the \textit{Unpatched + Filter} configuration, this resulted in a Max latency spike of 0.8032 ms (Table \ref{tab:master}).

The \textit{Patched + Filter (Hybrid)} configuration (Mode 2) represents the most resilient architecture. By combining the edge-layer circuit breaker with the application-layer patch, we achieve the lowest overall Mean latency (0.0150 ms). The circuit breaker handles over 90\% of the adversarial load with low overhead, while the official patch serves as a fallback to mitigate the impact of any packets that bypass the probabilistic sampling window. This defense-in-depth approach ensures that the emulated workload remains stable even under stateful adversarial traffic designed to probe sampling gaps.

\section{Discussion}

The experimental results validate the efficacy of the proposed Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker in maintaining deterministic QoS under structural complexity attacks. However, a robust systems-security evaluation requires a nuanced discussion of the mechanism's boundaries, inherent trade-offs, and practical deployment considerations within future MEC environments.

\subsection{System Jitter and Baseline Inspection Cost}
Traditional defenses require line-rate DPI, which imposes a constant processing overhead on every packet, increasing jitter for safety-critical V2X services. By using an FSM with probabilistic sampling, we amortize this baseline inspection cost. During nominal operations ($S_0$ state), the effective complexity is reduced from $\mathcal{O}(N)$ to $\mathcal{O}(R_{sample} \times w)$, where $w$ is the constant window size, minimizing the background resource footprint. This low-overhead filtering approach is critical for high-throughput edge nodes where compute resources are heavily contested.

\subsection{Tail Latency Control}
In V2X, the P99 tail latency is often more critical than the mean. Structural attacks specifically target the "tail," causing extreme outliers that can disrupt cooperative awareness. Our results show that the circuit breaker mechanism effectively clamps the tail latency, preventing HoL blocking from cascading through the parser processing pipeline. By discarding OER structural bombs before recursive expansion using the bounded-cost SSRF, the system ensures that legitimate time-sensitive packets are processed deterministically.

\subsection{False Positives and Generalizability Limitations}
The $\mathcal{O}(1)$ SSRF relies on the statistical improbability of long sequences of identical bytes in high-variation payloads like signed OER certificates. While highly effective for ASN.1 OER, this heuristic may exhibit false positives if applied to protocols that natively utilize extensive structural padding or zero-byte initialization. The repetition threshold ($\theta$) must be carefully tuned to the empirical distribution of the target protocol. Furthermore, this mechanism is strictly a parser-layer defense; it does not provide universal protection against semantic logic flaws, cryptographic vulnerabilities, or broad volumetric DDoS attacks.

\subsection{The First-Miss Penalty and Hybrid Defense Strategy}
A fundamental limitation of any probabilistic sampling defense is the "first-miss penalty." As observed in the \textit{Unpatched + Filter} scenario, stateful adversarial traffic can exploit the sampling gap during the $S_0$ state, resulting in a minor latency event before the FSM transitions to an elevated risk state. To address this, we strongly advocate for a hybrid defense strategy. Deploying the edge-layer circuit breaker to filter the vast majority of the adversarial load, while maintaining application-layer static recursion limits as a fallback, provides optimal parser pipeline resilience. This ensures that the rare packet bypassing the sample window is caught by the deterministic parser constraint, effectively suppressing the maximum latency spike.

\subsection{Stateful Adversarial Behavior and Deployment Feasibility}
Advanced adversaries might employ "low-frequency" or stateful attack patterns designed to trigger the circuit breaker just frequently enough to maintain an elevated risk state, thereby forcing the node to incur the SSRF inspection cost continuously. However, as demonstrated in our complexity analysis, the $\mathcal{O}(1)$ operation is computationally inexpensive and non-allocating. Therefore, even under a sustained stateful attack, the system experiences only a minor baseline shift rather than severe HoL blocking. This confirms the system's ability to provide \textbf{adversarial workload containment}, where the computational cost of the defense is decoupled from the algorithmic complexity of the attack. The practical feasibility of deploying this mechanism in MEC-enabled V2X ingress pipelines is high, given its minimal memory footprint and compatibility with existing protocol stacks.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}
Internal validity may be impacted by the choice of the 5\% sampling rate and the recovery budget parameters, which were empirically determined for the evaluated OER workloads. External validity is bounded by the use of the Vanetza protocol stack and the specific certificate structures defined in IEEE 1609.2. While the SSRF-based detection logic is generalized for OER recursive patterns, its performance on non-OER encoding rules remains a subject for future verification.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

While the proposed Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker effectively mitigates structural complexity attacks, several limitations present avenues for future research.

First, the $\mathcal{O}(1)$ SSRF relies on a statically defined repetition threshold ($\theta$). While effective for OER, which typically exhibits high bit-stream variation in legitimate cryptographic payloads, applying this heuristic to other protocols or encoding rules may require dynamic threshold tuning to prevent false positives. Future work will investigate adaptive thresholding mechanisms based on real-time traffic analysis.

Second, while the current implementation operates efficiently within the software ingress pipeline, the initial $\mathcal{O}(1)$ scan could be further optimized. Migrating the structural pattern detection to hardware-accelerated environments, such as FPGA smartNICs or eBPF/XDP frameworks, could provide true line-rate filtering before the packets reach the user-space processing queue.

Finally, sophisticated adversaries may attempt to bypass the defense using "low-frequency" stateful attacks designed to stay below the probabilistic sampling rate. Investigating advanced state-tracking algorithms that correlate structural anomalies across extended temporal windows remains a critical area for ongoing security engineering.

\section{Conclusion}

The integration of MEC into V2X networks introduces critical vulnerabilities at the presentation layer, where algorithmic complexity attacks can exhaust parser resources and induce severe Head-of-Line (HoL) blocking. This paper presented a Parser-Layer Circuit Breaker designed to protect V2X infrastructure from ASN.1 OER structural complexity attacks. By combining an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ bounded-cost SSRF with a Probabilistic Risk Budget FSM (PRB-FSM), we demonstrated a defense mechanism that preserves QoS through \textbf{adversarial workload containment}.

Our results confirm that by shifting the defensive objective to \textbf{bounded computational exposure}, the system can effectively decouple presentation-layer security from the algorithmic complexity of the payload. The proposed Hybrid Defense strategy reduces P99 tail latency from 0.1793 ms to 0.0320 ms under high-intensity structural attacks, ensuring that safety-critical V2X services remain deterministic. This systems-security approach provides a practical, low-overhead framework for securing high-throughput vehicular processing pipelines against parser-layer resource exhaustion.
