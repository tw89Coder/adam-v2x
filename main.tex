\begin{abstract}
Edge computing infrastructure faces severe service availability degradation when incoming data streams introduce algorithmic-complexity workloads capable of unbounded computational amplification. Existing defenses often rely on stateful inline inspection, which mitigates these threats but violates the strict microsecond latency budgets of resource-constrained edge nodes. Instead of maximizing inspection accuracy at the cost of performance, we propose a decoupled edge admission architecture designed to preserve service availability under bounded resource budgets, thereby mitigating queue buildup and tail-latency amplification under bursty workloads. By formulating computational amplification as a structural concentration problem, we establish a fundamental entropy--depth tradeoff under physical MTU constraints. This enables a zero-allocation, $\mathcal{O}(1)$ fast-path admission mechanism that leverages the $F_2$ frequency moment for inline structural filtering. To prevent inspection fatigue during nominal workloads, an asynchronous control plane utilizes a Finite State Machine (FSM) and Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) to optimize sampling rates dynamically. Using ASN.1 OER recursive parsing as a motivating case study, we demonstrate that our framework maintains bounded fast-path overhead and reduces P99 tail latency from 0.2229 ms to 0.0035 ms under severe workloads, ensuring resilient Quality of Service (QoS) for real-time edge applications.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The edge computing paradigm is defined by strict resource constraints and microsecond-level service availability requirements. However, when processing heterogeneous, multi-protocol data streams, edge nodes frequently encounter unbounded computational tasks—such as deeply nested serialization structures—that consume excessive CPU cycles. These localized computational spikes induce severe Head-of-Line (HoL) blocking, degrading the Quality of Service (QoS) for all subsequent time-sensitive edge applications. Protecting edge availability requires neutralizing these computational anomalies before they exhaust finite processor resources.

Existing defenses often rely on stateful inline inspection (e.g., Deep Packet Inspection or complex validation logic) directly in the data plane. However, such inspection introduces significant processing latency that inherently violates the performance budgets of edge systems. To maintain edge service availability, a defense architecture must not prioritize theoretical detection accuracy over system performance; instead, it must guarantee deterministic, $\mathcal{O}(1)$ execution overhead. This necessitates a fundamental shift from stateful protocol-specific inspection to stateless structural admission control.

To achieve this, we propose a decoupled edge admission architecture that separates fast-path structural filtering from slow-path policy optimization. We formulate computational amplification as a physical concentration problem, allowing the edge node to identify anomalous resource-consuming payloads using streaming algorithms. By treating recursive parser amplification (e.g., CWE-674) as our motivating case study, we instantiate our architecture on an intelligent transportation edge testbed.

The primary contributions of this paper are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Formulating the edge admission problem as availability preservation under bounded resource budgets and proposing a decoupled fast-/slow-path architecture;
    \item Deriving the entropy--depth tradeoff under MTU constraints, providing theoretical support for an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ structural admission fast path;
    \item Demonstrating empirically that the architecture mitigates queueing delay and tail latency while maintaining bounded fast-path overhead.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem Formulation}

\subsection{CWE-674 Multi-Protocol Bottleneck}
Recursive structures are endemic to widely used serialization schemas \cite{Ref_OER_Edge_2024}. When an edge node receives a payload with anomalous nesting depth, the presentation-layer parser attempts to allocate objects and traverse the schema tree \cite{Wang2024}. In an algorithmic complexity attack, an adversary injects syntactically valid but deeply nested structures constrained only by the Maximum Transmission Unit (MTU). This exploits the structural markers of the protocol (e.g., choice indices or container lengths) to exhaust CPU cycles prior to semantic validation.

\subsection{Queueing Theoretic Impact of Algorithmic Complexity}
The systemic impact of these computational spikes on edge service availability is best modeled using an M/G/1 queue. According to the Pollaczek-Khinchine (P-K) formula, the expected queueing delay $W_q$ is given by:
\begin{equation}
W_q = \frac{\lambda E[S^2]}{2(1-\rho)}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is the arrival rate, $\rho$ is the average CPU utilization, and $E[S^2]$ is the second moment (variance) of the service time $S$. An algorithmic-complexity payload acts as a computational bomb that exponentially inflates $E[S^2]$. Equation 1 mathematically demonstrates that even if the aggregate CPU utilization $\rho$ remains strictly bounded, this massive variance alone guarantees severe Head-of-Line (HoL) blocking, causing unbounded tail latency for all subsequent legitimate edge tasks. Neutralizing this variance in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ time prior to parsing is therefore fundamental to preserving service availability.

\subsection{Limitations of Traditional Defenses}
Conventional mitigation strategies rely on Deep Packet Inspection (DPI) or static compiler patching \cite{Zhang_Edge_2018}. However, DPI involves heavy signature matching that inflates the baseline inspection cost ($C_{inspection}$). Moreover, managing bespoke inline DPI rules for every potential protocol format (JSON, YAML, Protobuf, ASN.1) is computationally prohibitive for a unified edge ingress layer \cite{SmartNIC_2019, Sadiq_DataPlane_2023}. 

Our security objective is to design a protocol-agnostic pre-parsing filter that operates in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ time relative to packet length, decoupling the defense from specific protocol state machines. We base this on the insight that malicious structural repetition sharply contrasts with the higher entropy of legitimate data.

Recently, Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) have been proposed for adaptive cyber defense in edge and IoT networks \cite{SACD_2025}. However, conventional ML-based security typically deploys deep neural network classifiers directly in the data plane to perform inline packet classification (e.g., scanning every packet's payload features), introducing latency overheads that violate sub-10 ms V2X constraints. In contrast, our architecture decouples AI decision-making from the inline packet path: the C++ data plane strictly executes the lightweight, $\mathcal{O}(1)$ frequency sketch, while the DRL agent runs asynchronously on the slow-path control plane at coarse-grained epoch intervals, updating the sampling parameters without adding any processing delay to the ingress pipeline.

\section{Theoretical Foundation (Entropy--Depth Tradeoff)}

The theoretical foundation of our defense relies on the principle of structural marker concentration. Recursive payloads necessarily pack repeated protocol-specific structural markers into the MTU budget. If an attacker attempts to evade statistical detection by injecting high-entropy bytes between these markers, they inevitably consume the available physical packet capacity, thereby limiting the maximum achievable recursion depth. 

To formalize this physical boundary, we must establish the transport-layer conditions of vehicular networks. Crucially, IEEE 802.11 standards explicitly disallow MAC-layer fragmentation for broadcast and multicast frames. Since V2X safety messages (e.g., CAM and DENM) are transmitted exclusively as unacknowledged broadcast frames under the ITS-G5 protocol suite, payloads cannot be fragmented across multiple physical frames \cite{IEEE80211_2020}. This structural transport guarantee, rather than a mere simplifying approximation, allows us to define the following system assumptions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Assumption 1 (Finite Packet Size / MTU Limit):} The communication link has a strictly bounded Maximum Transmission Unit ($M$), and all parsed payloads must fit within a single physical frame.
    \item \textbf{Assumption 2 (Online Processing):} The edge node processes incoming packet streams on-the-fly, disallowing stateful, multi-pass deep packet inspection due to microsecond-level latency constraints.
    \item \textbf{Assumption 3 (Legacy-Compatibility Recursive Parsing):} The vulnerability exists within a retained legacy-compatibility parsing path in the decoder. While the current protocol schema is flattened and depth-bounded, backward compatibility with pre-flattened certificate chain grammars requires unbounded recursive-descent TLV traversal. The attack surface persists strictly due to this legacy code path, not the active deployed schema.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Theorem 1 (Entropy--Depth Tradeoff under MTU Constraints).}
\textit{
Let $M$ denote the effective MTU of the underlying link and let $S_{\mathrm{syn}}$ be the minimum structural bytes required for one recursive encoding layer. To ensure that no sliding inspection window of size $w$ simultaneously contains two adjacent recursive markers, the spacing between consecutive markers must satisfy $L_{\mathrm{spacing}}\ge w$.
}

\textit{
When random cryptographic bytes or entropy padding are used as the spacing mechanism to dilute structural concentration, the required padding satisfies $S_{\mathrm{entropy}} \ge L_{\mathrm{spacing}} \ge w$.
}

\textit{
Therefore, the maximum achievable recursion depth is physically bounded by:
}
\begin{equation}
D_{\max} \le \frac{M}{S_{\mathrm{syn}}+S_{\mathrm{entropy}}} \le \frac{M}{S_{\mathrm{syn}}+w}.
\label{eq:entropy_depth_bound}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Proof.}
If the spacing between two adjacent recursive markers satisfies $L_{\mathrm{spacing}}<w$, there always exists a sliding inspection window that simultaneously contains both markers, exposing the repeated structural pattern to the statistical detector. Therefore, successful evasion requires $L_{\mathrm{spacing}}\ge w$. When random bytes are used to realize this spacing, the required padding satisfies $S_{\mathrm{entropy}}\ge L_{\mathrm{spacing}}\ge w$. Since every recursive layer occupies at least $S_{\mathrm{syn}}+S_{\mathrm{entropy}}$ bytes, a recursion depth of $D$ requires a payload of $D(S_{\mathrm{syn}}+S_{\mathrm{entropy}})$ bytes. Because the payload must fit within a single transmitted frame, the size is bounded by the link-layer MTU ($M$). Hence, $D(S_{\mathrm{syn}}+S_{\mathrm{entropy}}) \le M$, which directly yields Eq.~(\ref{eq:entropy_depth_bound}).
\hfill$\blacksquare$

Theorem 1 establishes that recursive amplification inevitably manifests as localized structural concentration under finite message budgets. Therefore, detecting localized collision concentration becomes sufficient for identifying malicious recursive payloads. Among available streaming statistics, the second frequency moment ($F_2$) provides an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ estimator of concentration without logarithmic computation, making it suitable for edge deployment.

This entropy--depth tradeoff is the cornerstone of our defense. It guarantees that any structurally concentrated recursive payload will be detected, while any payload padded with sufficient entropy to evade detection will be too shallow to cause CPU exhaustion.

\subsection{O(1) Streaming Structural Risk Filter}
To identify anomalous structural concentration, we utilize the second frequency moment ($F_2$) as an engineering primitive \cite{Alon1996}. The $F_2$ moment is a classic streaming algorithm metric that estimates collision concentration in bounded memory. We compute the localized $F_2$ score, denoted as $SQ$, over a sliding window of size $w$:
$$SQ = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{V}} c_j^2$$
where $c_j$ is the frequency of byte value $j$ in the window. 

For legitimate high-entropy payloads, $c_j$ is highly dispersed, resulting in a low $SQ$. Conversely, recursion bombs inflate the $F_2$ moment due to concentrated structural markers. 

To prevent false positives from natural structural padding in standard protocols (e.g., unpopulated optional fields creating \texttt{0x00} sequences), the $SQ$ calculation explicitly excludes the \texttt{0x00} state. By isolating the detector strictly to non-zero structural frequencies, we effectively eliminate non-semantic padding noise without compromising security, as triggering a CWE-674 uncontrolled recursion inherently requires non-zero structural markers (e.g., choice indices or active bitmasks) to force the parser into deeply nested loops.

\begin{algorithm}[!ht]
\caption{Bounded Streaming Structural Risk Filter}
\label{alg:bssrf}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Payload $P$, window size $w$, scanning limit $L_{max}$, threshold $\theta$
\State Initialize frequency sketch $H[256] \gets 0$
\State Initialize fixed-size ring buffer $W[w]$ and index $idx \gets 0$
\State $SQ \gets 0$ \Comment{Localized $F_2$ moment}
\State $n_{scan} \gets \min(|P|, L_{max})$

\For{$i = 0$ to $n_{scan} - 1$}
    \State $b \gets P[i]$
    \If{$i \ge w$}
        \State $b_{old} \gets W[idx]$
        \State $SQ \gets SQ - (2H[b_{old}] - 1)$
        \State $H[b_{old}] \gets H[b_{old}] - 1$
    \EndIf
    \State $W[idx] \gets b$
    \State $H[b] \gets H[b] + 1$
    \State $SQ \gets SQ + (2H[b] - 1)$ \Comment{Amortized $F_2$ update}
    \State $idx \gets (idx + 1) \pmod{w}$

    \If{$i \ge w$ and $SQ > \theta$}
        \State \Return \textbf{True} \Comment{Structural anomaly detected}
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \Return \textbf{False}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm \ref{alg:bssrf} updates $SQ$ in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ time per byte. By limiting the inspection to a parametric maximum length $L_{max}$ and utilizing a threshold $\theta$, the filter overhead is strictly $\mathcal{O}(1)$ relative to the packet length, requiring constant memory and zero dynamic allocation. This C++ data plane implementation ensures hardware-level optimization suitable for latency-critical paths.

While the $\mathcal{O}(1)$ filter is highly efficient, continuously inspecting all traffic can lead to inspection fatigue on resource-constrained nodes. We introduce a Probabilistic Risk Budget Finite State Machine (PRB-FSM) to dynamically modulate the inspection sampling rate based on a continuous risk budget $B_t$ \cite{Sosnowski2024}. Budget thresholds (normalized design boundaries, e.g., $\tau_1=70$, $\tau_2=40$) map the continuous budget to discrete operational states ($S_0 \dots S_3$) with escalating sampling rates. Importantly, because these thresholds represent a normalized reference scale, their specific numeric values are arbitrary scaling choices; the DRL agent in the cognitive plane automatically calibrates its learned policy parameters (e.g., the base sampling rate) to adapt to these boundary states, rendering the system's dynamic control invariant to absolute threshold scales.

To automatically tune this sampling budget under non-stationary traffic, we model the traffic adaptation problem as a Constrained Markov Decision Process (CMDP) \cite{Altman1999_CMDP} optimized by a Deep Q-Network (DRL agent) \cite{Mnih2015_Nature}. The objective is to minimize inspection overhead while bounding exploit leakage. The agent uses a Lagrangian-constrained reward function \cite{Ref_Lagrangian_DRL_2023}:
\begin{equation}
R_t = - c_{\text{overhead}} \cdot p_{\text{inspect}} - \lambda_t \cdot \max\left(0, R_{\text{leakage}} - \epsilon_{\text{target}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $p_{\text{inspect}}$ is the sampling probability, $R_{\text{leakage}}$ is the exploit leakage rate, and $\lambda_t$ is the dynamic Lagrangian multiplier.

Crucially, this architecture decouples asynchronous policy optimization from the latency-critical data path. The data plane maintains a hardcoded Layer-1 Overrun Guard that immediately overrides the DRL policy to 100\% sampling if the threat budget drops precipitously \cite{Nygard2007}. This ensures data plane credibility and deterministic lower bounds on security performance regardless of DRL exploration noise.

\section{Experimental Evaluation}
The entropy--depth analysis motivates the use of a bounded structural concentration metric in the data plane. The following experiments evaluate whether this theoretically motivated design achieves the intended systems objective—preserving availability under constrained CPU budgets.

\subsection{Experimental Testbed and Traffic Modalities}
The testbed utilizes a 12th Gen Intel i7-12700H and 16 GB of RAM running Ubuntu 22.04 LTS. Single-core pinning is adopted to isolate algorithmic CPU overhead from multi-core scheduling effects, establishing a controlled baseline for resource-constrained edge environments. The software is based on Vanetza\footnote{Vanetza C-ITS Suite: \url{https://github.com/riebl/vanetza}}, an open-source ETSI C-ITS suite processing CAM/DENM payloads via the \texttt{asn1c} compiler\footnote{asn1c (Mouse Fork): \url{https://github.com/mouse07410/asn1c}}.~To evaluate the defense, exploit payloads were constructed via structured fuzzing targeting the legacy-compatibility certificate-chain parsing path in Vanetza. It is important to note that this vulnerable path coexists with a newer depth-bounded parser, and no CVE has been assigned to this specific finding to date. We integrated a custom QoS evaluation framework into the ingress pipeline to measure presentation-layer latency. Crucially, although the evaluation runs on an x86 platform, the $\mathcal{O}(1)$ algorithmic complexity and zero-allocation memory design of the filter provide strong architectural, instruction-set-independent advantages. We hypothesize that the instruction-set-independent nature of the $\mathcal{O}(1)$, allocation-free design may yield comparable relative efficiency gains on ARM-based AIoT platforms; hardware-specific evaluation on such platforms is explicitly left to future work.

To establish a realistic baseline, we extract nominal background traffic from the V2AIX dataset \cite{10920150}. We interleave this real-world traffic with 1,000 mutated recursive malicious payloads across multi-scale injection rates ranging from 0.1\% to 10.0\%. The system is evaluated under four scheduling configurations: Mode 0 (Continuous Uniform), Mode 1 (Single Strong Pulse), Mode 2 (Periodic Pulsing). We measure the Mean, Median, P99, and Max Latency to quantify queue delay and Head-of-Line (HoL) blocking.

\subsection{Edge Workload Amplification and Filter Efficacy}
To quantify the vulnerability, we profiled the unpatched parser under MTU-constrained structural attacks. In our isolated micro-benchmarks, we observed that parsing latency exhibits strict linear growth ($\mathcal{O}(N)$) relative to recursion depth, bounded only by the physical MTU. To profile the worst-case scenario, a single MTU-saturating exploit payload (approx. 1400 bytes) was measured to consume approximately 960 $\mu$s of CPU time. As predicted by the Pollaczek-Khinchine equation (Eq. 1), this millisecond-scale anomaly exponentially inflates the service time variance $E[S^2]$. In real-time AIoT networks, such localized computational spikes instantly induce Head-of-Line (HoL) blocking, degrading the QoS of subsequent safety messages.

Under these workloads, our deployed pre-filter successfully isolates malicious structural concentration from benign structural padding. By rejecting payloads that violate the theoretical entropy--depth bound, the filter maintains a 0.00\% False Positive Rate (FPR) across all clean test sets while enforcing $\mathcal{O}(1)$ execution boundaries, thereby preempting the inflation of $E[S^2]$.

This stability is enforced by the FSM's hysteresis-based recovery, which mandates a clean streak of 1,000 packets before restoring the lower sampling policy, preventing oscillation exploits and bounding the transient "first-miss penalty" spikes.

\section{Conclusion}
This work proposes a theoretically grounded, ultra-lightweight streaming detector for recursive parser amplification that is practical for resource-constrained edge systems. By formulating the vulnerability as a structural concentration problem, we derived the fundamental entropy--depth tradeoff under physical MTU constraints, demonstrating that attackers cannot simultaneously evade detection and achieve deep recursion. Leveraging the $F_2$ frequency moment as a low-overhead engineering primitive, our decoupled C++ data plane filter provides $\mathcal{O}(1)$, zero-allocation inspection. Complemented by an adaptive RL-driven sampling framework, our approach successfully prevents CPU exhaustion and reduces P99 tail latency from 0.2229 ms to 0.0035 ms under severe amplification attacks. This architecture delivers deterministic threat containment without inflating baseline latency, ensuring resilient QoS for critical edge applications. Furthermore, the underlying vulnerability class—generic recursive-descent parsing over broadcast, reassembly-free transports—plausibly manifests in industrial protocols such as IEC 61850 GOOSE, whose Layer-2-only Ethernet transport structurally precludes IP-level fragmentation \cite{IEC61850_Goose_L2, Fuzzing_IEC61850}, suggesting the broad applicability of our defense beyond intelligent transportation systems.
